What It Is For
- This file does not explain what the project is.
- It explains where the project really lives, which entrypoints are real, which points attract but lead you away, and which surfaces have high impact if touched.
- It exists to give a concrete reading of the real body of the project, not of its idea.

What Goes Here
- Active repos or folders. They belong here because they show where the project really lives. If you close them well, you gain immediate technical orientation.
- Real entrypoints. They belong here because they show where you truly enter the project. If you make them clear, you gain safer access.
- Places not to start from. They belong here because they prevent wrong or too-costly entry. If you name them well, you gain fewer mistakes.
- Sensitive files and surfaces. They belong here because they show where a small change can have large impact. If you make them visible, you gain more control.
- Minimal runbook. It belongs here because it gathers only the minimum required to start, verify, and make a first safe touch. If you keep it lean, you gain practicality.
- Critical env, config, or dependencies. They belong here because they explain what truly changes the project's behavior. If you close them well, you gain more reliable technical reading.
- Critical external integrations. They belong here because they show what the project touches outside its own files. If you make them readable, you gain fewer surprises.

What Does Not Go Here
- Project identity. That belongs in 01 because it explains what the project is, not where it lives technically. Otherwise you risk turning 08 into an orientation file. If you place it in 01, you gain cleaner boundaries.
- Hard-to-reopen constants. That belongs in 02 because it belongs to the stable constraints of the project. Otherwise you risk mixing technical topology and principle. If you place it in 02, you gain a more concrete surface map.
- Current live work. That belongs in 03 because it describes the present operational state. Otherwise you risk making 08 a disguised working doc. If you place it in 03, you gain a more stable map.
- Decisions already taken. That belongs in 04 because it records already-won choices. Otherwise you risk using 08 as a technical decision log. If you place it in 04, you gain more clarity.
- Ideas, backlog, or latent material. That belongs in 05 because it is not yet closed. Otherwise you risk inflating 08 with directions that are still open. If you place it in 05, you gain a more readable map.
- Full manual, long setup, or extended troubleshooting. Those do not belong here because 08 should remain a minimal map, not total documentation. Otherwise you risk making it heavy and difficult to maintain. If you keep them out, you gain reading speed.
- Total inventory of everything. That does not belong here because not everything matters in the same way. Otherwise you risk filling the file with noise. If you keep only the critical, you gain a map that is truly useful.

Update It When
- The real topology of the project changes. Update here when the place where the project really lives changes. This avoids stale maps that send people to the wrong place.
- An entrypoint changes. Update here when the real entry path changes. This avoids first access starting from the wrong place.
- A sensitive surface changes. Update here when a point changes class of impact if touched. This avoids unaware changes in high-consequence zones.
- A critical dependency changes. Update here when something external or configurational truly changes the project's behavior. This avoids wrong technical readings.
- The minimum required to start, verify, or touch the project safely changes. Update here when the first safe move is no longer the same. This avoids old or dangerous entry habits.
