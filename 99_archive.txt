What It Is For
- This file holds what is no longer alive.
- Put here things that are closed, superseded, duplicated, or dead if you want to keep them as trace.
- It exists to preserve memory without letting finished things weigh on the present.

What Goes Here
- Superseded versions. They belong here because they no longer guide the project, but may still matter as trace. If you place them here, you gain memory without noise.
- Duplicates. They belong here because they should not weigh twice on the present. If you archive them, you gain more clarity.
- Closed snapshots. They belong here because they describe a past state, not work that is still alive. If you place them here, you gain a cleaner present.
- Dead ideas. They belong here because they are no longer in play, but you may want to keep a trace of them. If you archive them, you gain less confusion.
- Things that no longer have a live role but may still matter as historical memory. They belong here because they should not guide the present. If you place them here, you gain a useful archive.

What Does Not Go Here
- Things still useful but out of time. Those belong in 98 because they may return soon. Otherwise you risk archiving too early something that is not truly finished. If you place them in 98, you gain more flexibility.
- Things still alive and still open. Those belong in 05 because they still matter now. Otherwise you risk killing a line that is still active. If you place them in 05, you gain a cleaner archive.
- Things already inside current work. Those belong in 03 because they are part of the current live state. Otherwise you risk removing from work something that is still guiding the project. If you place them in 03, you gain more clarity.
- Decisions already taken and still valid. Those belong in 04 because they are not dead history. Otherwise you risk pushing into the past a choice that is still active. If you place it in 04, you gain a more correct archive.

Update It When
- The live role of something is truly closed.
- Something should no longer weigh on current choices.
- You want to preserve a trace without leaving it in the present.
- Something archived remains useful only as memory, not as a live line.

Typical Errors
- Archiving too early just for visual cleanliness.
- Putting here things that are still ambiguous but alive.
- Using archive as a substitute for parking, latent, or decision.
- Confusing historical memory with work that is still active.
